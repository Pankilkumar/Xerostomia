Dry Mouth (Xerostomia)

Xerostomia

Dry mouth can happen from dehydration or anxiety.
When it stays dry all the time, it may point to an underlying health condition.
Untreated xerostomia can increase tooth decay, gum disease and other dental issues.

Overview

Occasional dry mouth can happen when you're dehydrated or feeling particularly anxious.
Dry mouth all the time is xerostomia and can point to an underlying health condition.
Left untreated, xerostomia makes you more vulnerable to tooth decay, gum disease and other dental issues.

What is xerostomia?

Xerostomia is the sensation you may feel when you don't have enough saliva.
Salivary glands produce saliva to lubricate your mouth.
Saliva helps keep the mouth clean and comfortable.

Key dental context

Occasional dry mouth is normal, especially when your mouth is temporarily dry from dehydration.
Chronic dry mouth means your mouth is dry all the time.
Dry mouth is common, affecting about 1 in 5 people.
Your risk for dry mouth increases with age.

What Causes Xerostomia?

Xerostomia happens when something causes your salivary glands to produce less saliva.

Underlying health conditions.

Health problems that cause dry mouth

  • Salivary stones (sialolithiasis).
  • Salivary gland inflammation (sialadenitis).
  • Diabetes.
  • Alzheimer's disease.
  • Stroke.
  • Sjogren's syndrome.

Myth vs Fact

Dry mouth is easy to ignore, but chronic xerostomia can affect comfort, eating, speech and dental health.
Knowing the facts helps people seek care before tooth decay, gum problems or mouth sores get worse.
Myth: Dry mouth is just thirst.
Fact If your mouth stays dry even when you drink water, your salivary glands may not be producing enough saliva.
Myth: It only affects comfort.
Fact Chronic dry mouth makes you more vulnerable to tooth decay, gum disease, mouth sores, cracked lips and oral thrush.
Myth: You should wait it out.
Fact Persistent dry mouth is a reason to call a healthcare provider. Early diagnosis and treatment can reduce symptoms before they worsen.

Diagnosis and Tests

A dentist looks for signs of xerostomia during an examination.
Your tongue and inner cheeks may look dry and textured rather than smooth, shiny and moist.
To see how much saliva you produce, a dentist may dab around your salivary glands with gauze.
They also look for cavities and gum disease, which can both occur as a result of dry mouth.

Management and Treatment

The main goal of dry mouth treatment is to increase saliva production.
Improving saliva flow helps reduce the risk for tooth decay and gum disease.

Underlying cause

Specific dry mouth treatment depends on the underlying cause.

Dry mouth products

Products made for dry mouth can help reduce discomfort.

Saliva stimulators

Saliva production stimulators like Cevimeline (Evoxac) and Pilocarpine (Salagen).

Surgical repair

Surgical salivary gland repair (less common).

Outlook / Prognosis

You can successfully manage dry mouth symptoms with treatment.
In some cases, xerostomia may go away completely.
Most of the time, it takes trial and error to figure out a solution.

Prevention

There's not a miracle cure for dry mouth, but there are things you can do to reduce your risk.
Most importantly, practice good oral hygiene and visit your dentist regularly for cleanings and exams.

Try these dry mouth remedies at home

  • Chew sugarless gum to stimulate saliva production.
  • Suck on ice cubes or sugarless ice pops.
  • Use a mouth wash formulated for dry mouth.
  • Avoid decongestants and antihistamines when possible.
  • Use a cool-mist humidifier, especially if you breathe through your mouth at night.
  • Keep lip balm handy.
  • Drink plenty of water.

You should try to avoid

  • Smoking.
  • Carbonated drinks.
  • Beverages containing alcohol.
  • Acidic, spicy, dry and sugary foods.

Living With

Everyone gets dry mouth on occasion.
When you have persistent dry mouth, it's time to call a healthcare provider.
Early diagnosis and treatment can reduce symptoms before they worsen.

Questions to ask your provider

  • What's causing dry mouth?
  • Do I have an underlying condition that needs treatment?
  • How often should I have teeth cleanings?
  • What dry mouth products do you recommend?

Why is my mouth dry even though I drink a lot of water?

If your mouth still feels dry after drinking plenty of water, you might have xerostomia.
Your salivary glands may not produce enough saliva, so your mouth still feels dry even when you stay hydrated.

A Note

It's normal for your mouth to feel dry every now and then.
If you have dry mouth all the time, you could have xerostomia.
Over time, dry mouth can affect your teeth and gums.
See a healthcare provider at the first sign of persistent trouble.
